## Biography
Joe Williams, born Joseph Lee Williams on December 12, 1918, in Cordele, Georgia, moved to Chicago at age three and grew up immersed in the city's vibrant music scene on the South Side. As a youth, he sang with a gospel group and began his professional career at 17 with the Johnny Long Orchestra. By 1937, he joined Jimmie Noone's band, followed by stints with Les Hite, Coleman Hawkins, Lionel Hampton (1942-1943), Andy Kirk, Red Saunders, and others, while working odd jobs like doorman at the Regal Theater to support himself. His rich baritone voice excelled in jazz, blues, and ballads, blending smooth delivery, wit, and emotional depth.

Williams's breakthrough arrived in 1954 when Count Basie hired him as the male vocalist for his orchestra, replacing Jimmy Rushing, a role that earned him the nickname 'Count Basie's #1 son.' From 1954 to 1961, he propelled the band's resurgence with hits like 'Every Day I Have the Blues' (1955), 'Alright, Okay, You Win,' 'Going to Chicago,' and 'The Comeback,' featured on the album *Every Day*. His smoother style contrasted Rushing's aggressive energy, expanding Basie's repertoire into ballads and blues. After leaving Basie, Williams launched a prolific solo career, recording over 45 albums with artists like Cannonball Adderley, George Shearing, Harry Edison, and the Thad Jones–Mel Lewis Orchestra, while touring extensively in nightclubs, festivals, and on TV.

Williams's legacy endures as one of jazz's premier male vocalists, known for his versatility and enduring appeal. He won a Grammy in 1984 for *Nothin’ but the Blues*, appeared in films like *The Moonshine War* (1970) and TV shows including *The Cosby Show*, and continued performing until his death on March 29, 1999, in Las Vegas, Nevada.

## Fun Facts
- Joe Williams received a $50 money order from Count Basie in 1954 with instructions to join the band in New York, launching his stardom after sitting in during an intermission at Chicago's Trianon Ballroom.
- Nicknamed 'Count Basie's #1 son,' Williams first worked with Basie's octet in 1950 before rejoining the full orchestra in 1954.
- Despite early fame, Williams worked as a backstage doorman at Chicago’s Regal Theater in the late 1940s, meeting jazz greats while pursuing music part-time.
- He acted in the 1970 film *The Moonshine War* and guest-starred on TV shows like *The Cosby Show* (1985–92) and *Johnny Carson*, blending music with entertainment.
